How do you tell the story of a rainforest with all of its immensity, abundance, and ferocity? How do you grapple with an inheritance mired in blood, violence, trauma and loss? Fierceland is an evocative tale that tends to these questions with grace, nuance and sensitivity. It’s also probably my favourite book of 2025 (!!). Equal parts family saga and eco-horror, the novel follows two siblings, Roz & Harun, interrogating the legacy of their palm oil baron father and how it has indelibly shaped them.  
  
“green is the sprawl, rot & fern-tickle of time...” There’s an unflinchingly visceral quality to the writing: where an assemblage of perspectives coalesce into each other, where scenes can feel simultaneously hallucinatory yet vivid. Under Musa’s pen, the Sabah rainforest isn’t just a passive backdrop where pillaging, logging, and palm oil cultivation takes place. Rather, it’s a character in its own right: polyphonous, effervescent, mesmerising, and shifting. Ghosts are everywhere–not just the ghost of Roz and Harun’s father, but also the omnipresent ghosts of the rainforest, its people, and its languages. I remain so very enchanted by his depiction.  
.𓈒𖡼𓂂𓏲𓆸܀  
At its core, Fierceland confronts big themes of environmental extractivism, indigenous dispossession, political corruption, censorship and complicity. It’s also ultimately a return to an age old truth — that we don’t lead our lives in silos! That we exist in relation to land, inheriting its losses, histories and ecologies,,That our existences are so very tethered to each other, human and more-than-human alike 🕸️
