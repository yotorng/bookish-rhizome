a cyberpunk eco-thriller by Chen Qiu Fan

Waste Tide is set in a land well-concealed from our consciousness, residing beneath the veneer of a glossy, purportedly frictionless global economy. This land is Silicon Isle, the final destination of the world's discarded e-waste. Concentrated in a town located within a South China coastal province, e-waste imports are divvied up by three clans that effectively wield all economic power.

An underbelly of working-class rural migrant workers prop up this obfuscated economy of e-waste recycling. Derisively termed as waste people, they toil away in toxic, sprawling junkyards, manually extracting precious metals and plastics from electronic products at the tail-end of their lifespan.

And yet — for all its mangled familiarity and echoes of our world's late-stage capitalism, Waste Tide is decidedly cyberpunk.

In a hedonistic, hyper-consumerist fashion, humans can choose to upgrade their bodies and senses through digital prosthetics. Chen conjures a world where technology is irrevocably enmeshed with the corporeal form, and interrogates how that might play out in a not-so-distant future.

The story follows a whole slew of characters. We meet Scott, a representative of a US recycling corporation attempting to 'modernise' Silicon Isle's operations, and meeting great resistance from the local clans and government. Kai Zong, Scott's translator, is a third-culture Chinese American invested in learning more about Silicon Isle and his roots. He builds a relationship with Mimi, a 'waste person' hoodwinked into forced labour.

When a digital prosthetic device unleashes a mutant AI/virus software onto Mimi, Silicon Isle's tenuous state of affairs tips into chaos.

At its core, Waste Tide is an evocative and gripping novel that interrogates power, capitalism and technology.

>“But no one really cares. The natives don’t care. They just want to squeeze as much money as they can out of whatever life is left in this place. The migrant workers don’t care, either. They just want to earn enough money as quickly as possible to return to their home villages and open up a general store, or build a new house and get married. They hate this island. No one cares about the future of this place. They want to leave here and forget this period of their lives, just like the trash.”

With haunting precision and depth, Chen depicts the gritty, rugged lives of Silicon Isle’s inhabitants. The island feels eerily familiar, precisely because it is created in the likeness of places that already exist in the peripheries of our global economy.

Chen weaves a powerful tale of the invisibility of waste in our modern lives — and the creeping, systemic manner this is enforced.

Waste Tide’s political climate brims with immensity, capturing class conflict between migrant workers and the natives, the clans’ power strife, the state's rule and occasional acquiescence, the neo-colonial dynamics of Scott's US company, and its transnational forces of exploitation and extraction. These power differentials are also deftly encoded through the varying uses of Mandarin, Topolects (方言), and English.

>“Mimi saw even more: the lonesome, the gamblers, the addicted, the innocent . . . ​hiding in brightly lit or dark corners of the city, worth millions or penniless, enjoying the convenient life brought about by technology, pursuing stimuli and information loads unprecedented in the history of the human race. They were not happy, however; whatever the reason, it seemed that the capacity for joy had degenerated, had been cut off like an appendix, and yet the yearning for happiness persisted stubbornly like wisdom teeth.
>
>Mimi felt a wave of pity for them, civilization’s favourite children.”

Chen’s portrayal of technology is mostly sombre and macabre. But I think his argument harks back to a well-worn theme. Just as we make technology, it, in turn, irrevocably shapes us —in all of our propensity for “joy, sorrow, terrors and passions”.

For all its highlights, my main gripe(!) is that violence enacted on women was the main vehicle moving the plot forward. Despite Chen's rich world-building and incisive story, the character of Mimi (as the sole female lead) falls flat, reduced to an almost cliche portrayal of a helpless, innocent victim of her circumstances.

All in all, Waste Tide is a thunderous, riveting novel that I thoroughly enjoyed. But its flat take on Mimi’s character undermines part of its message, doing it a disservice.
