Book by Vincent Delecroix, translated by Helen Stevenson

>*There is no shipwreck without spectators. Even when there's no one, when it's far out at sea, at night, without witnesses, even when there's no living soul in sight for thousands of nautical miles, only waves and the viscous night, covering everything, swallowing everything; when there are no more eyes to see than there are arms to reach out, there are still spectators and the shore from which they are watching is never far away, even if, at the same time, it is infinitely distant.*
>
>*Even with their eyes shut, people are still watching, and I can't think of a single one who could say: I wasn't there. From inside their own homes, they are all watching the drama, and the drama is never-ending; it plays out every day, every night, on high days and holidays, even when they're doing other things, they're still spectators of the 'drama at sea'.*

“You will not be saved”, utters our narrator, a French coastguard radio operator, to an asylum seeker dialling to her for help as their inflatable dinghy slowly capsizes in the English Channel. Small Boat is based on the largest single (preventable) loss of lives in the Channel, with 27 migrants drowning in November 2021 despite numerous calls to both British and French coastguards.  
  
We delve into the psyche of our unreliable narrator, as she turns herself in for questioning by the police. She’s defiant, obstinate and detached, insisting that she carried out her role to the best of her professional abilities. I’m reminded of Hannah Arendt’s banality of evil — an evil without gleeful villains, but instead embedded within our indifferent institutions. Where French or English waters are an arbitrary line in the sea, imagined by nations yet leaving devastating implications on how rescues are doled out.  
  
And yet — it might be tempting to assume that this book assigns its readers moral authority to judge our narrator. All that is casted aside as the novel progresses.  
  
“You will not be saved.” It become increasingly clear that the narrator is not just addressing the migrant on the phone, but appealing to us as a passive reader, an armchair observer. That we are all complicit spectators, that none of us are truly absolved of this unjust horror. A harrowing, but necessary read.
