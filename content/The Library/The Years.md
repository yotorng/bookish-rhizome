Book by Annie Ernaux, translated by Alison L. Strayer 

>*Our photographs and films - filed by date and viewed onscreen, one after the other without a pause - were pervaded by the light of a time that was unique, no matter how diverse the scenes and landscapes. Another form of past came into being, fluid, with little real memory content. There were too many images for us to stop at each and recall the circumstances in which they were taken.*
>
>*Inside them, we lived a near weightless, transfigured existence. The signs of our existence multiplied and put an end to the sensation of time marching on.*

>*As she waits at the hypermarket check-out, she occasionally thinks of all the times she's stood in line with a cart heaped with food. She sees the vague silhouettes of women, alone or with children circling their cart. They are faceless, distinguished only by hairstyle — a low chignon, hair cut short, in a bob, or loose and medium-long— and clothing — the seventies maxi-coat, the black midi-coat from the eighties. She sees them as images of herself, taken apart and separated like matryochka dolls.*
>
>*She pictures herself here in ten or fifteen years with a cart filled with sweets and toys for grandchildren not yet born. But she sees that woman as improbable, just as the girl of twenty-five saw the woman of forty, whom she has since become and already ceased to be.*

*“our memory is outside us, in a rainy breath of time”🌧️*  
  
The Years is a French memoir by Annie Ernaux that is largely written either in third person (“she”) or in the collective pronoun (“we”), spanning from post World War II 1941 to modern day 2006. It’s simultaneously universal and singular, sprawling and specific, historical and contemporary all at once.  
  
🌫️*“All the images will disappear.”* Ernaux writes vividly and almost urgently at times, capturing fragments of various cultural milieu through everyday depictions: a dinner conversation, a photograph, a particular piece of film or TV show or song, offerings at the supermarket, advertisements, and so on. Personal memory melds with political history, and her words conjure (what must feel like) the zeitgeist of a generation.  
  
📷🫧 History feels almost visceral, slippery and alive. Time is seemingly a main character, bearing witness to shifts in modernity, nationhood, family, gender, class, technology, and consumerism. The memoir feels like an ode to life itself, where in Ernaux’s own words: *“history is not an optional extra.”* Truly a book that makes me feel small in the most expansive way possible! ❤️‍🔥✨🪞🎞️
