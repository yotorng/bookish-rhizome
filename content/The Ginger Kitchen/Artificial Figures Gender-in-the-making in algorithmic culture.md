# Artificial Figures: Gender-in-the-making in algorithmic culture
A public lecture by NTU Humanities, by Dr Thao Phan

## Darmouth, 1956, the 'birthplace' of artificial intelligence as a field
>"We [propose](https://ojs.aaai.org/aimagazine/index.php/aimagazine/article/view/1904) that a … study of **artificial intelligence** be carried out during the summer of 1956 at Dartmouth College in Hanover, New Hampshire. The study is to proceed on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." — J. McCarthy 'inventing' the field of AI at Dartmouth
- Comprised of mathematicians and information studies researchers, including John McCarthy, Claude Shannon, Marvin Minsky, and Nathaniel Rochester
- framed the artificial intelligence problem as that of *making a machine behave in ways that would be called intelligent if the human was so behaving*
- marked a breakaway from the prevailing cybernetic model of computation
	- Which focused on modelling brains in computer hardware
- Towards methods that abstractly **mimic human minds in software**
	- Some research topics suggested over the course of summer included:
		- how to use English as a base to construct an artificial language for programming action,
		- how to teach a machine to create an abstract model of sensory environments, 
		- how to use information theory to explore questions of noise and brain models and represent environments using mathematical structures,
		-  how to strategically introduce randomness to encourage a machine to solve problems that require originality
- Not only gave the field its name, but also helped us **solidify the key questions, key frictions, key social patterns and relations that would come to define the field for the next three decades**

What we might call a **figurative moment in AI's contemporary history**:
- Figurative as in *allegorical*, serving to create a certain imagination
> *...to retrospectively help to establish the metaphor of birth, the allegory of coming into being, of mummies, or in this case, daddies, who labor to produce an imagined body, a body who would not exist without the labor.*
- Figurative in providing an *image or representation of the field*
	- Figure's latin root of *figura*, referring to shape, form, figure
	- Figure used to describe a person's *outline*, or a *graph or image*
> *...the shape of the field, the image of an AI practitioner, the representation of a body, in this case, a body of knowledge or field of expertise of certain technologies...*
- This kind of figuration is **propositional**
	- describing something before it arrives, 
	- does more than just bring into existence, it creates its own existence
	- giving the impression that the field always, already was 
- these allegorical stories naturalise the birth of AI, AI as a clear, coherent, and present thing in the world that has an obvious form
>*...as if AI isn't actually a completely dispersed field of techniques systems, tools, actors, interests, imperatives, labor processes, legal processes that occur at different times in different places...that only sometimes become legible to us when they take the shape of a a device, or interface, or a company, or a CEO, or a fictional character or some other kind of speculative object.*
- in this lecture, **they'll be using these figures and figurative moments to explore how in the making of AI figures, gender, too, is being made**

## In the making of AI, gender too is being made.
- Beginning with the assumption that there is a relationship between the way we conceptualise systems (such as gender), and the way we conceptualise technological phenomena (such as AI)
- While gender unquestionably shapes and structures scientific objects and knowledge, rarely do we consider *how these phenomena have the capacity to shape gender in return*.
- Gender often reduced to predetermined, preformed category, already made, rather than something that is constituted through the practice of science or technology-making
- Aim is to demonstrate:
	- how gender fundamentally constitutes how AI figures are made intelligible as subjects and objects,
		- Gender is what mediates expectations and make the role of AI clear to user, consumer or audience
	- As technologies are figured by gender, gender becomes technologised
		- Gender is figured as an artifice, as a performative and imitative category that even a machine following the correct procedures can also enact 
- grounded in feminist STS - 
	- A field of women's research that treats science as objects of study
	- What counts as scientific knowledge and why?
	- Who is given authority in these important knowledge making communities and why?
	- what is the role of science and technology in actively constituting categories like gender, race, ability, class, and nation?
	- Studying what Bruno Latour calls 'science-in-action'
		- latour argues that too often, science and technology is obscured behind a black box, one that conceals the very complexity of actors and networks that function to present situated propositions as objective matters of fact.
		- we study "science-in-action", and not ready-made science or technology
		- To do so, we either arrive before the facts and machines of the black box, or we follow the controversies that open
		- Objective: observe science as it is in the making, in progress ; in order to trace the myriad of investments in our industry, power struggles that underpin claims produced through the scientific process
		- calls STS researchers to shadow scientists in the field, calling it necessary to disrupt the trope of the hero scientist who independently discovers the world, and instead give due recognition to the various actors (human or otherwise) that contribute to the **fundamentally social and cultural practice that is the construction of scientific knowledge**.
	- Studying **what other formations are in the making, while science is being made**. 
	- Studying what Donna Haraway also calls 'gender-in-the-making'
		- gender as a structure in relation and regulatory apparatus
		- "Following gender, then, is not just about following preformed beings and examining either their presence or absence at the scene, assigned to the action, but rather treating **gender as something that is constantly being made and remade in the institution of power that is science** 
		- Understanding gender as a contingent and ongoing;
		- in line w Judith Butler, gender as a dynamic relation, constantly constituted through a performing of acts that could only ever be examined in the making
- How might AI, like gender, also be constantly be under construction?
	- How can we locate the site of where AI is being made?
	- Technological figures are not predominantly made in the lab per se, but rather across multiple sites, in scientific discourse, popular culture, commercial media, policy, journalism, and other sites in everyday life.
- AI is no single figure with no single birthplace.


## Figure 1: Alan Turing
- the face of another person who has been deemed as a founding father of AI, a key figure in AI's cultural history
- recognized within the scientific community for his contributions to the development of early electronic computing, his theoretical in inquiries into the nature of machine intelligence, and for his work during the Second World War as a code-breaker for the British government.
- one of the very few early AI figures to receive mainstream recognition ; but also lived and died on the discriminatory legislation against homosexuality and homosexual acts
- Turing test/imitation game: considered the first official benchmark for evaluating machine intelligence
	- exploring this moment as a moment of rigid categorisation, of dismissals and erasure, 
	- as a moment in which these exclusion become entrenched within future agendas and imaginaries of AI, 
	- and as a moment in which human-machine difference is modelled on gender and sexual difference
- First introduced by Alan Turing in his 1950 article titled [Computing Machinery and Intelligence](https://courses.cs.umbc.edu/471/papers/turing.pdf)
- Begins by asking can machines think? -> proposes an experiment that explicitly uses as an activity that speaks to differences between men and women
	-  based on a 19th century parlor game 
	-![[Screenshot 2025-12-11 at 2.25.24 PM.png]]
- (1) The first official test for determining machine intelligence is explicitly modeled on a **game of drag and gender crossing**.
	- When men pretend to be women, women defend their status as women, success is a matter of feminine passing and a gay time is had by all
- (2) This delightfully queer scene is so often forgotten or dismissed or erased from this foundational moment
	- When we move from the imitation game to the Turing test, questions of gender and sexuality, of identity and bodily difference, indeed all questions of embodiment (period) are swept aside **in favour of a model of intelligence that reduces the complex functions of thinking and communication to processes of simple manipulation and mimicry**
	- There's a long and established critique of the difference between the mind and the body, where especially in Western intellectual tradition, the thinking subject is characterised as disembodied, operating through pure reason, unencumbered by the corrupting forces of the flesh
		- The primary criticism is that the hierarchy between body and mind mimics hierarchies of gender, such that the feminine is constantly associated with the more devalued term
	- We can argue then,as many have, that the **Turing test aligns the science of AI with an intellectual tradition that is historically framed gender and the gendered body as a constitutive other.**
- Although the Turing Test and the disembodiment that it presents has been heavily critiqued by researchers, it continues to influence AI imaginaries today -  **a deep state of belief in the disembodied nature of the AI subject** that allows outlandish claims within a computational culture
	- E.g. Elon Musk aiming to upload his consciousness onto a machine
- For Turing, the distinction between the mind and body, between the intellectual and the physical, would be a recurring theme in both his theoretical work and also in his life.
	- Concerned with math,  the symbolic and the abstract -> whcih led to him excelling in cryptoanalysis
	- A profession not only qualitatively identical to form with mathematics, but also structurally at the institution he was affiliated with, one that demanded a bifurcation of self (inside and outside)
	- Working with the govt decoding top secret messages required discretion, but in the sense of being discreet(secret), but also in discrete (Separate and apart from others)
